The SSLC 525 Shift: Is the Two-Language Policy a Silent Threat to Tulu?

By Kabi Alupendra | Editor-in-Chief

BENGALURU/UDUPI— The Karnataka Department of School Education and Literacy has just changed the rules of the game. By reducing the SSLC total marks from 625 to 525 and turning the 3rd language into a "grade-only" subject, the state has effectively moved toward a two-language formula. While this reduces the immense pressure on students, it opens a deeper debate regarding the status of regional languages like Tulu.

The Death of the "Scoreable" 3rd Language

For decades, the 3rd language (often Hindi or Sanskrit, but also Tulu and Urdu) was a mandatory 100-mark paper that factored into a student's final percentage. Under the new 2026 guidelines, students will still sit for the exam on March 31st, but they will receive an A, B, C, or D grade instead of marks. These grades will not influence their aggregate score or their ability to pass 10th grade.

The Tulu Vacuum

Our recent RTI research at The Tuluva Guardian has already highlighted a worrying trend: zero enrollment in Tulu as a 3rd language in districts like Kundapura and Brahmavara, despite a 100% pass rate in other blocks. By removing the "marks" incentive, we fear that school administrations will discourage students from taking Tulu, treating it as a non-essential hobby rather than a core academic pillar of our heritage.

"Policy is the reflection of priority. If our language is not 'worth' marks in the eyes of the state, it risks becoming invisible in the eyes of the student."

A Call for Recognition

The Tuluva Guardian calls upon the education ministry to ensure that the 3rd language shift does not lead to the total erasure of regional electives. We propose that Tulu be considered for elevation to 2nd language status in the coastal belt to reflect the linguistic reality of our students.

The Tuluva Guardian will continue to monitor the March 31st exam proceedings and report on any further policy shifts.
